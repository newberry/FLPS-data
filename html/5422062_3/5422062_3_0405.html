Pride has two manifestations. The first is a refined feeling born in a human being from a feeling of worthiness and dignity. It comes when a duty is conscientiously performed, from patriotism, from love for our fellow-man and from a striving towards all that is good.

The second kind of pride springs from ignorance, egoism, disregard of others and boldness, sources all of the wrong kind of pride. The first pride is a virtue, the second a vice. National or patriotic pride is what we call that feeling which a good person has in his heart when he sees the progress of his country or hears the praises bestowed on the sages of his country.

This feeling of pride is aroused in the breast of every Greek when he 2visits the Illysium, where there is an exhibition of art, sculpture, and painting. There--among the great creations, such as "The Reapers," the painting by Henri Marten, and "A Doctor in the Medical Academy," by Wiseman--we find the works of Greek artists.

The Greek creations are so good that they had to be accepted by the super-critical Committee of Selection of the Exhibition.

Four works of Mr. Emanuel Kavarou, who studied in America where he won a prize and then continued his studies in Paris, were accepted by the Committee.

Another reason for national pride comes from the words heard at the meeting of a club which is being formed to encourage the study of Greek in France. Some of the philhellenes (Friends of the Greeks) in this 3organization are Paul Girard, Mason, Pone, Fouzer, and Bourge.....

They said that Greece achieved her unity through the efforts of Venizelos, and that thanks to him she is now ready to welcome the inquiring spades of the archeologists.

The work of Venizelos at the Spa Conference, concerning the matter of Ipiros, will be another justification for our national pride.
