In a ceremony as ancient as classic dancing and as modern as radio broadcasting, Hellenic and American civilizations met yesterday at the dedication of the Greek pavilon at Chicago's 1934 World's Fair.

Preceding a program of Grecian music and ballet centuries old, Charalambos Simopoulos, Greek minister to the United States, broadcast concerning the world's interest in America's economic fight.

Envoy Broadcasts

Witnessing the ceremony in the court of states, 15,000 Chicagoans of Greek origin cheered minister Simopoulos' statement:

"Every visitor looks with amazement and admiration at the progress accomplished by your country, the achievement of your industry and your development of the sciences.

"We all follow with the utmost interest the fight of the American people and its government against the world crisis and we look to America as the leader to world prosperity."

Points to old Glories

Pointing to the efforts of various nations to unearth the glories of ancient Greece, he added:

"Greece welcomes this international collaboration, inspired by the idea that the treasures of the classical period constitute a precious heritage for all humanity."

John D. Dritsas, acting Consul General of Greece, praised the accuracy with which the beauties of Greece had been reproduced for the fair exhibit. Sounding the welcome and appreciation of the exposition was President Rufus C. Dawes.

To be welcomed with booming guns and military honors this morning will be Wilehelm Morgenstierne, Norwegian minister to the United States, who arrived in Chicago last night.
