Chicago's German Citizens: "Chicago's 800,000 Germans are famous for their solid and successful citizenship. The remarkable things about the German in Chicago is that here all the political lines are down in a pan-Teutonic feeling," recently said Harry Rubens, president of the German Mannerchor. "Germans from Austria, Prussia, Switzerland or Germans from Germany, we all stand for better citizenship, as Germans for Chicago."

Carl B. Roden, whose German birth and early training combined with his splendid work in the Chicago Public Library, has given him a keen insight into the German at work as a Chicagoan, carried this thought further in a conversation with the writer.

"Your true Chicago German," said Mr. Roden, "is first of all a good citizen of the republic, loyal, intelligent, law abiding, not usually a meddler, an

agitator or a critic of American institutions. He is here not so much a seeker of fortune as of these liberties which our institutions offer and which he found too much hedged about in his former home. He accepts and enjoys these liberties gratefully and with that inborn respect for constituted authority which is one of his strongest traits.

"If he does not feel inclined to rattle the eagle's feathers neither does he feel called upon to let morality, love of order, and joy of life. His loves and penates are 'sittlichkeit and 'gemutlichkeit', and these may, perhaps, be set down as his chief contribution to American civilization. There is scarcely a department of human activity in America to which Germans have not added their share with honor to themselves and profit to the nation. Chicago would not be the great musical center that it is today without its German citizens. It can barely have retained the right to its name of 'Garden City' were it not for the Germans' love of a garden. But chiefly he has helped us to find ourselves by pouring upon the seething waters of our American life the oil of his stability, his reverence for things as they are and the powers that be, his respect for amenities, moral and political as well as social, his 'sittlichkeit' [gap] so comprehensive that the lord chancellor of England has used it untranslated to connote the whole subject of national and international comity.
