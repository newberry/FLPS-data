In last Saturday's supplement of the Chicago Examiner, on art and literature, Art Editor Marcus gave Einar Hansen's, "Portrait of Mrs. F," quite a write - up. He writes:

"I lingered for a long time by Einar Hansen's "Portrait of Mrs. F," and I returned several times. This picture did not get the award, but it did receive a ribbon for honorable mention.

"I was told by the attendant that this picture had received more public comment than any other picture exhibited.

"Mrs. F. is a tired, unfortunate figure with red hair, and wearing a white 2dress. A woman overworked, without any hope for the future, probably a miner's wife. There is unusual pathos in the bowed head, and the long, helpless hands. I have seen women like her around the death shaft at the Cherry Mine, after the cave in; I have seen women like her among the people in Shanty Town in Muncie, Indiana, and among the striking oil-workers in Bayonne, New Jersey.

"Hansen's picture is well done, the sketch is excellent. His picture reminds me of Carl Melcher's "Holland Woman," but the Chicago artist works with simpler subjects."

The portrait is also reproduced in the Examiner, with the caption "A much talked about portrait."

3

This picture, which received honorable mention in the Chicago Artists' Exhibition at the Art Institute, is, again we repeat, the most talked about picture at the Exhibit. It is a pathetic portrayal of tiredness and helplessness before the Gods of destiny that rule human existence.

Danes that have not as yet had the opportunity to see this excellent picture, should do so at once.
