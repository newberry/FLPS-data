When our visitor first entered the City Editor's office, he was pleasantly surprised at being confronted with a man who, according to his understanding of psychology, was possessed with all the qualifications that make for greatness.

After a short exchange of greetings, he being offered a seat, the visitor explained to the City Editor his reason for calling.

Lately, one of the big stores in town had been having trouble with their help, all due to the fact that the store owners, who already were paying their help starving wages, were trying to cut the amount down to a minimum, unacceptable even to a slave.

\2

The City Editor, with his kind eyes and well formed forehead, sat patiently listening to the plea for justice, so eloquently expressed by the spokesman, and representative of the working girls. After the visitor had finished his plea, the editor stated that his sentiment was for the girls one hundred per cent.

Yet, when asked if he would accept an article which would bring the real facts home to the public, the Editor frankly admitted the impossibility of such a thing.

The spokesman left, but his first and favorable impression of the City Editor was somewhat dimmed. He asked himself the question; why such a noble head had been placed on a spineless body?

After weighing the incident however, the spokesman realized that the City Editor himself was but a slave, who, for the sake of a job, had to sell his soul. Poor Editor.
