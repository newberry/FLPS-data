It is not the first time that we are tearing the mask off that whole American advertise, "prosperity." More than once have we warned the working fraternity not to allow themselves to be deluded with promises of great. That "tomorrow will always remain tomorrow" for us, and the "today" for the American workers is hard bitter, unmerciful, uncertain and miserable.

Humbug will not help when the truth stares into one's eles. In this country it is becoming worse from year to year, life if becoming harder right along. In order not to appear hopeless pessimists, criticizing and condemning all without exception, we will call upon sources most likely competent because they come from the United States government itself. In an address of Uncle Sam's Marines Recruiting Bureau, endeavoring to induce volunteers to join its ranks, we read word for word:

Young man, think, what do you possess today and what kind of future is 2before you, and you will see what great opportunities are offered to you by the Marines. Work is uncertain, strikes, lay-offs of the workingmen and sickness. Working yourself up to a better position goes slowly, and favoritism is in use very often. The work is small and limited after you learn a trade. Every day is the same monotony, the same tormenting toil. The place of work is gloomy, dreary and uninteresting. When you get sick the pay stops and the doctor's bills start. If you become disabled, unable to work or crippled, you receive very little or nothing at all. In case of death your family receives what you were able to save from, your small earnings. Almost all your income goes for livelihood. Old age is nearing and sickness; you saved very little money, your job was taken by a younger person and a more active man. Behold! What it promises you?"

Unfortunately that is the way it truly is, and even much worse. This country in many ways has remained far behind the rest of the world, even if it hides carefully these deficiencies under an outward pretext of false showing; but in no other direction are we most likely more backward and 3negligent than in the matter of work, in our industrial country. Under the pretense of not wanting to bind personal liberty, the workingman has directly been taken from under the law, statutes have nor been provided for, regulating the relations of the workingman with the employer, protecting and insuring financially the weaker worker and his family. Neither of them is sure of tomorrow, health, or life. At any moment without reason can he be thrown out of work; misfortune, injury or death lies in wait for him in the factory or mine, for the economy of the "boss" deprived of protective means and conducted in a frantic way.

Prosperity! Not long ago, when the (debate on) limitation of immigration was going on in Congress, an argument was raised there that the immigrants are lowering the standard of life of the American workingman.

Oh! Pharisees, shameless bluffers! We are curious how a workingman with a family can live on a higher standard with this from-day-to-day growing, unheard of high cost of every day needs of life, with this 4constant shrinking value of that sacred dollar, for which he must work so hard, and which he hardly is allowed to smell because at once with a thousand bricks and methods, bordering up on robbery, it is scared out of his pockets by slickers. Some time ago, as is told by older immigrants, this was possible, it was well-being when a workingman could earn several dollars a day, and everything was cheap, when one would live adequately with a family on three to four dollars a week, when meat cost four cents a pound and everything was cheaper in proportion. Today articles of every day life are more expensive here than anywhere else, and the demands are greater right along, even for bare necessities. The workingman can work his hands down to his elbows before he can save anything from his earnings; only the most powerful and the most attentive, at the same time the most conservative, and depriving themselves of the least little things, are able to accomplish that. The rest of them live from day to day, eternally bare, often in debt according to the maxim, "Matthew earned it, Matthew ate it."

Because that is what is particularly wanted, that is what is desired 5by our bosses, capitalists and enterprisers. Toward this aim also is adapted our entire machine, our entire social system. It seems as if there was intentionally contrived thousands of attractive methods of spending money. The people have been driven into an ambition for sports, there has been propagated a mania for sports, there has been propagated a mania for dressing up trifles, and the throwing away of money on useless and unnecessary silly things. The thrifty workingman, having some sort of small capital, begins to value his own worthiness, does not allow himself to be treated with contempt, or quits his hard, murderous labors, start his own business or goes on a farm; he has to be kept in a state of continuous life from day to day, continuous want and dependence on the boss and his capital, in a continuous working yoke.

Prosperity today already belongs to the past, and small is our hope that it will again so soon arrive in this country. We have a few kings, true financial self-possessed tsars who govern this country with the help of several millionaires; the remainder are their subjects, the millions of dark masses, working hard for profits for them (the millionaires).

Self-dependent trade has been killed by the great manufacturing industry, and merchandising business. Farming is on the graces of trusts and the companies, the workingman, a slave of capital. Beyond the slim handful of the wealthy, there are no people or independent classes. There you have prosperity, which so faithfully even if unintentionally, is being characterized by this address to the volunteers.

Years will pass before these conditions will change for the better. There remains nothing else for our Polish immigration but to save themselves with self-help. Let us have as much as we possibly can, let us deprive ourselves of all kinds of luxuries, let us live modestly, not put ourselves out for sports and pomp. Let us stick together, as it becomes brothers, let us organize and insure ourselves in Polish societies, let us help each other in business. Let us not allow ourselves to be fooled with idle talk about prosperity, when poverty is already vexing us and maybe a greater depression can come. Money, that is our only weapon, our worth and our deliverance in this country, whose brand is uncertainty of tomorrow and life.
